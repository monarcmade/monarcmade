### 1:15 The mistake most beginners make
The biggest mistake beginners make is learning AI randomly.

One day it’s ChatGPT prompts. The next day it’s agents. Then it’s automation. Then it’s coding. Then it’s some new tool that just launched.

That creates motion, but not progress.

If you want AI to become a real skill, you need to learn it in layers.

### 2:00 Stage 1: Prompting basics
First, learn how to talk to AI clearly.

Not fancy prompts. Clear prompts.

You need to know how to give context, define the role, explain the task, set constraints, and ask for a specific output.

Example: do not say, “help me market my business.”

Say, “Act as a content strategist. My audience is small business owners who want AI automations. Give me 10 YouTube video ideas with hooks, titles, and a business CTA.”

That is the difference between playing with AI and using it.

### 2:45 Stage 2: Workflows and context
Second, learn workflows.

AI is not just one prompt. The real value comes when you can use it across a process.

For example: brainstorm video ideas, turn one idea into a script, turn the script into slides, turn the slides into Shorts, turn the Shorts into LinkedIn posts.

That is a workflow.

Once you understand workflows, AI starts saving you real time.

### 3:30 Stage 3: Automations
Third, learn automation.

This is where AI starts connecting to your actual work.

Think: form submission comes in, AI summarizes it, creates a task, drafts a reply, and sends you a Slack message.

That is when AI stops being a chatbot and starts becoming part of your business.

You do not need to automate everything. Start with one boring task that repeats every week.

### 4:15 Stage 4: Agents and tools
Fourth, learn agents.

An AI agent is basically AI that can reason through a goal, use tools, and complete steps with some level of independence.

But do not start here.

If you skip prompting, workflows, and automation, agents will feel like magic and break like magic.

The right question is: what tools should the agent use, what data can it touch, and when does a human need to approve the work?

### 5:10 Stage 5: Projects that sell
Fifth, build projects that prove you can use AI in the real world.

Do not just build cute demos.

Build things like:
- an AI content planner
- a customer support draft assistant
- a lead qualification workflow
- a business document chatbot
- a meeting summary system
- an invoice or intake automation

These are projects people understand. These are projects businesses pay for.

### 6:00 What I would do in the first 30 days
If I had 30 days, I would do this:

Week one: prompting and clear outputs.
Week two: workflows and content systems.
Week three: automations with real business tasks.
Week four: one complete AI project that I can show people.

That is the roadmap.
